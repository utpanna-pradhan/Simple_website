Guide to become a web developer

*Choose Your Development Specialization

Generally speaking, most developers fall into one of three categories: front end, back end and full stack.

1·Front End Development

If you work in front end development, you manage the user experience for people who visit a website. Front end developers are experts in CSS, HTML and JavaScript, and can create the code that brings website design ideas to life. Your tools define how users interact with a website; if you love the creative, visual side of web development, a front end career may be a great option for you.

2·Back End Development

Back end developers design, maintain and improve the technologies that keep sites running behind the scenes. Back end developers use languages such as Python, PHP, Ruby on Rails and Java to build applications, manage server-side technologies and interact with front end code for faster, more responsive website experiences.

3·Full Stack Development

You don’t necessarily have to choose between front and back end programming. If you have a strong aptitude and interest in both aspects of web development, you may pursue a career as a full stack developer. These professionals are knowledgeable about all aspects of the web development process, from conducting back end, server-side programming to managing front end user experience. This path may also be appealing to new career changers who don’t want to limit themselves early on in the educational process. For that reason, many coding bootcamps provide an array of full stack skills to give you a strong foundation in most major areas of development.

*Keep Learning and Coding

Web development is an incredibly dynamic field, with new technologies, preferred frameworks and standards continually evolving. Even after you become a web development professional, one of the best ways to ensure your continued success is to keep learning and coding, exploring newer technologies and building upon your skills. According to recent reporting from Stack Overflow, roughly 75 percent of polled professional developers say that they learn a new technology “at least every few months or once per year.” Open-source technologies can be a great way for web developers to build their portfolios, strengthen their coding chops and network with others in the field. For example, sites like GitHub allow people to contribute to large, open-source projects and collaborate with other programmers. Good contributions and commitment to these projects can help introduce your work to others in the industry and build an even more diverse and technically rich portfolio. Like the projects you produce, a coding career is dynamic, interactive and continually evolving to suit the times. By remaining active in the community and sharpening your skills, you ensure that your skills stay marketable and relevant to employers’ needs.

*Keep Your Skills Strong

Many tech industry jobs involve a technical interview or a coding test. Even after you graduate, keep practicing and building projects. Beyond creating a great portfolio, this practice will keep you in good shape when it’s time to take a technical interview or ace a coding test. The good news is that web developers and coders are in high demand. Many employers are looking for web professionals who have the skills they need to keep their sites running and develop new applications. By keeping these tips in mind, you will be well-placed for a quick, successful career shift.

*Develop a Great Resume and Portfolio

Writing a great resume can highlight your technical skills and specialties for prospective employers at a glance. Action-oriented resumes that highlight your achievements and direct employers to your portfolio can help you get attention from hiring managers and show off your technical skills.

*Work Your Network

Networking is crucial for success in the tech industry. When you attend an online coding bootcamp or college program, you’ll likely develop connections with many other industry professionals. When you join message boards, open-source development groups or even social clubs, you should let people know that you are in the market for a job. When people are impressed by your work, dedication and technical skills, they just might refer you to the perfect opening for the next step in your career.

*Coding Bootcamps

Online coding bootcamps can be excellent resources for people who value flexibility and want to upskill quickly. In a bootcamp, students focus more on web development skills that employers are looking for in comparison to a more theoretical approach that college curricula typically offer. Bootcamps focus on teaching foundational and intermediate web development skills. Many bootcamp curricula are tailored to a particular area of focus (i.e., front end, back end, full stack). That said, you can expect to leave a full stack web development bootcamp with job-ready coding knowledge and a well-stocked professional portfolio. Full stack bootcamps are full, structured programs that provide experienced instructors, individualized support and lively class environments. They are particularly attractive for their flexibility; aside from being significantly cheaper than four-year degrees, many provide part-time night scheduling options that can be completed in as little as three to six months.
